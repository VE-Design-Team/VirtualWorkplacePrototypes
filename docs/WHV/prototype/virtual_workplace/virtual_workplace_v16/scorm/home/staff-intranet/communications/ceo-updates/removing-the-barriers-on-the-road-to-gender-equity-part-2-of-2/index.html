4. Removing the barriers on the road to gender equity (Part 2 of 2).

Following on from Part 1 on removing barriers on the road to gender equity, let’s now look at some of the practical things we can do in the workplace.

What are the practical things we can do in the workplace?

Let’s start with business structures and policies. The following aspects, of your core business, can support gender equity, but they can also face their own challenges.

  • Quotas
    Creating a practice to recruit/promote one gender over others may experience resistance to the cries of, “Shouldn’t it just be about the best person for the job regardless of gender?” or, “What about men’s rights?”. But quotas can be a practical way to address unconscious bias and privilege in the workplace that has consistently favoured one gender over others.
  • Procurement
    Consider how you procure products and services. Is it supporting a gender equity approach? And also consider your supply chain. How can your organisation use its influence to encourage gender equity in other businesses?
  • Marketing and Communications
    Strong brands are consistent with what they say and do. Any discrepancy between what you tell your external clients and what you tell your internal team will impact on your ability to create gender equality and brand trust. Again, your organisation can be a strong role model in the public arena.
  • HR policies (including recruitment, training and career development)
    Writing gender equity into your HR policies will provide a clear leadership for change. But they also have to be supported by change in the culture and behaviour of the organisation (more on that below).
  • Physical workplace structures
    Think about how the workplace is physically constructed to be inclusive of gender diversity. Some studies say how office temperatures are often set for men’s comfort, not women’s. Another consideration is how gender-neutral toilets can be a simple way to be inclusive and welcoming to gender diverse people. That can be as easy as changing a sign on a door. 

Now we come to the area that can sometimes be harder to measure, monitor and change, but is no less impactful; culture and behaviour. How you speak and act in the workplace is another way of living your core business and brand, so it should always be included in strategies for organisational change.

  • Leadership
    Obviously confident and supportive leadership is valuable when you are creating organisational wide change. And simple structural changes normalise cultural change, such as the gender-neutral toilets creating a more welcoming workplace.
  • Language
    Language is a powerful tool for cultural change and normalising new, inclusive ways of thinking. There are language guides to help you consider your approach.
  • Consultation
    Listening to, and engaging, the people in your organisation on this gender equity journey will go a long way to achieving success. My colleague Phillip Martin also talks about the value and process of consultation in empowering cultural change and inspiring agency in his blog article.
  • Stakeholder relationships and organisational reputation.
    Organisations often fear they will lose clients or business by focusing on gender equity. And that if they put themselves forward then others will see them as having a ‘problem’ with gender. It’s important for organisations to keep framing the issue of gender equality as being larger than any one organisation. Gender inequality affects all parts of society, so organisations that take the lead on addressing it are providing a service to the community. Consider this as an opportunity to become a leader in the field of gender equity in your industry and publicly celebrate your progress.

Ultimately change takes time and effort and creating gender equity in the workplace is no exception. However, we know the advantages of gender equity are too important to ignore as they can create a productive, innovative and successful business.

Helen Omondi,
CEO
