8 Collecting feedback for monitoring purposes

Work through the following scenario on collecting feedback for monitoring purposes. This is not an assessment. In each step, select the action you would take. You can try this activity as many times as you like.

The strategy to build a representative and diverse pipeline to leadership has been in place for six months. You are keen to work out if the strategy has been effective and if it is making a difference at GCBH.

Do you:

  • Go and ask a few people around the organisation for their opinion.
  • Reconvene the working group and begin a review process.

You speak to two managers, the receptionist and one of the male staff members from employment services.

They all say that the program seems to be going well, they note that one of the managers has come back from maternity leave part-time and is leading a team. The man from employment services acknowledges that he was afraid he would miss out on opportunities, but he still got to act up when his manager was on leave so he’s feeling okay about it.

Next you will:

  • Gather data on who has received mentoring under the strategy.
  • Reconvene the working group and develop an evaluation plan.

You reconvene the working group who helped advise you on the development of the strategy. The working group come up with a series of process and outcome measures to assess the impact of the strategy. They also develop targets to define what success would look like for the strategy.

Next you will:

  • Go and collect data in accordance with the process and outcome measures.
  • Gather data on who has received mentoring under the strategy, as this was the agreed first step.

You attempt to find out who has been mentored under the leadership pipeline strategy but the data doesn’t seem to exist. Although several managers claim to have mentored someone, no one has kept a record of who they mentored or how many sessions took place. When you ask managers for this level of detail they say they are too busy to go through their diaries and work it out.

Do you:

  • Leave it, it’s too hard to work out the detail and at least you know that mentoring happened.
  • Reconvene the working group to help you identify the best way to collect the data you need.

You comb the organisation for data on the various aspects of the strategy. Some data – like the current percentage of diversity among staff employed at different levels in the organisation – is relatively easy to find. Other things – like the number of people who have received mentoring – is proving difficult.

You ask the members of the working group to help you by advocating for the evaluation and supporting your data collection.

Eventually you have evidence to respond to each of the process and outcome measures defined by the working group.

Do you:

  • Report your results to the working group.
  • Present the results at an all staff meeting.

The rest of the strategy seems to be going okay and people seem to find it a sensitive topic so it’s better not to ask too many questions.

  • You have no evidence or clear understanding as to whether the gender equity strategy has been effective or if it is making a difference. Try again.

You present the evaluation results at an all staff meeting. You make it clear that from the data collected the Executive Management team have failed in their duty to implement the strategy and question their commitment to gender equality.

The Business Services Manager takes you aside after the presentation and expresses her disappointment. She says that you have made the Executive Management Team look bad in front of staff and that this has discouraged them from taking further action.

The CEO decides that the strategy is too contentious and takes it off their priority list.

  • By not going back to the working group, you have missed an opportunity to review and interpret the results to determine what aspects of the gender equity strategy has or hasn’t worked and what can be done next. Go back.

You report your evaluation findings to the working group. It seems that the strategy is yet to create any genuine change at the leadership levels of the organisation. Many of the activities identified in the gender equity strategy have been implemented in a piecemeal or sporadic way due to a lack of clarity about who is responsible for implementation.

The working group encourages you to present your findings to the Executive Management team, which you do. The Executive Management team thank you for you work and dedicate time to review the gender equity strategy together to clarify targets and responsibilities before sharing findings with the staff.

  • Congratulations! By engaging with the working group and other key stakeholders, you have been able to find evidence as to whether the gender equity strategy is having an affect and the best way to present these findings.
