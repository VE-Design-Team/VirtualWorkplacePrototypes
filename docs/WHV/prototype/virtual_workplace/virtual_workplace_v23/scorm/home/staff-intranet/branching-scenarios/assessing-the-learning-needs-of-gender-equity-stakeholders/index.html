4 Working with gender equity stakeholders

Work through the following scenario on working with gender equity stakeholders. This is not an assessment. In each step, select the action you would take. You can try this activity as many times as you like.

You are Gentown Council’s Diversity and Inclusion Officer, council has won funding to work with a community partner to embed gender equity across their organisation. GCBH has shown interest in partnering on the project and your job is to assess how ready they are to participate. Will you:

  • Get in touch with the CEO.
  • Send some information and see if they respond.

You ring the CEO of GCBH and ask to meet and discuss the gender equity process. The CEO is enthusiastic about partnering and invites you to come and present on gender equity to the board. You agree and start preparing your presentation. Do you:

  • Ask the CEO for information about the organisation’s mission and values to guide your presentation?
  • Go and produce a presentation based on one you delivered to council last year.

After a week you have had no response from GCBH. Do you:

  • Go to your manager and report that GCBH is not interested in the project.
  • Organise a meeting with a friend who works at GCBH.

The CEO gives you information on the mission and values of the organisation. She tells you the board members are a mixed group, some of them are likely to be resistant to gender equity but if you focus on the business case they are likely to support it. You incorporate the CEO’s feedback and present to the GCBH board, focusing your presentation on the benefits of gender equity for a flourishing workplace. At the end of the presentation most of the board seem supportive of gender equity action. One person expresses concern about “going too far” and “discriminating against men”. Do you:

  • Take this as a sign that GCBH is not ready to do this work.
  • Acknowledge the speakers’ point of view but focus on the support shown by the rest of the group.

You find the presentation you gave to the council last year and change the names to fit the GCBH board. You decide that the best approach is one that prioritises the prevention of violence against women because that’s what worked for council.

Your presentation to the GCBH board focuses on the link between gender equity and preventing violence against women. A number of the board members express doubt about the link or seem confused. One person states that violence against women is not part of the core business of GCBH so they can’t be expected to address it – several other board members nod in agreement.

After the meeting you let your manager know how it went and give your opinion that GCBH is not yet ready to tackle gender equity.

Your manager asks for more detail about the meeting and urges you to speak to the GCBH CEO again before you give up.

You organise a meeting with the GCBH CEO where she acknowledges the resistance at the board meeting but is still very keen to partner on the initiative.

She encourages you to speak with the Executive Management team and lets you know that a presentation that focuses on the business case for gender equity is most likely to convince them.

Do you:

  • Incorporate the feedback into your presentation and attend the Executive Management meeting.
  • Tell the CEO that you cannot commit any more time and you are going to focus your efforts on other partners that are ready to work with you.

You tell your manager that GCBH is not ready to participate in the gender equity initiative.

Your manager lets you know that they met the CEO of GCBH at a regional leaders’ forum and she seemed keen on promoting gender equity. Your manager encourages you to get in touch with the CEO’s office directly.

  • You get in touch with the CEO.

Your friend thinks that there might be interest in gender equity because it aligns with the organisation’s values. She recommends that you try contacting the CEO’s office directly.

  • You get in touch with the CEO.

You stop trying to engage GCBH and instead focus on other partners. Months later you discover that GCBH went ahead with developing a gender equity strategy without local government stakeholder input. 

  • By not taking on the advice or feedback of the CEO, you have missed an opportunity to work with a supportive community partner. Building a relationship with stakeholders takes time. Try again.

The CEO encourages you to speak with the Executive Management team and lets you know that a presentation that focuses on the business case for gender equity is most likely to convince them.

  • You integrate this information into your presentation and deliver it to the GCBH board.

You present the business case for gender equity to the Executive Management meeting. They are immediately supportive and start talking about developing a whole of organisation gender equity strategy. They identify that some staff will need further training to understand the importance of gender equity and why the GCBH has chosen to focus on it. After the meeting you talk to the CEO and both agree that the presentation was well received. The CEO praises the way you wove together the GCBH values with the business case for gender equity. The CEO puts gender equity on the agenda for the next Executive Management meeting and appoints a member of staff to carry out a review of GCBH policies. She invites you to get in touch after the Executive Management meeting to identify your next steps.

  • Congratulations! You have taken into account your stakeholder needs by meeting with the CEO and board members and integrating their feedback into your presentation to key staff. You have taken steps to develop a strong business relationship for your gender equity work.
