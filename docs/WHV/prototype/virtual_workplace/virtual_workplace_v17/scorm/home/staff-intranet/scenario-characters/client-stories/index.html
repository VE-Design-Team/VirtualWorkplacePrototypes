Client stories

Nyan Deng is a single mother of three, is in her early 20s, and originally from South Sudan.

Nyan recently separated from the father of her three children. After arriving in Australia last year, they moved interstate three months ago for her husband to take on a job. Nyan’s husband earned the family’s only income. He left the family two months ago, taking Nyan’s documentation.

Without any documentation, Nyan is unable to access any settlement support services and is struggling to determine what support she can receive from Centrelink. This is especially troublesome as Nyan has asthma and requires regular medication. She lives in a private rental that is in her husband’s name and the landlord is now demanding rent.

Due to her recent interstate move, Nyan does not have any contacts in the local community. To support her three children, Nyan cares for them during the day and sews at night for cash-in-hand work at rates below minimum wage. Nyan does not drive, have a mobile or SMART phone, nor does she know how to use a computer. She does not know how to use the public transport system and can’t afford to pay for cabs.

When Nyan was younger, her parents had the perception that educated women were less suitable for marriage. They preferred that she stay at home and take on more traditional roles within the family home. As a result, Nyan has limited formal education. She stayed at home and helped care for an elderly member of the family in South Sudan.

Nyan is very concerned about her future and how she will support herself and her family without the financial support of her husband. She is keen for her children to have a strong and positive educational experience here in Australia and has dreams herself, of returning to school and obtaining a qualification.

Mila is a 33-year old single woman. She injured her right leg in a car accident and has restricted use due to muscle damage. Mila needs a cane to support her when she walks and receives a disability support pension.

Mila originally started art classes as a form of stress management and rehabilitation following the accident. She now attends because she enjoys painting and the social aspects of meeting people in the classes. Mila has also sold some of her art work at the Art Centre and wonders if she could make art her career.

Mila has attended a few of the GCBH Employment and Training Programs but has struggled to secure long-term work since her injury. She has only been able to occasionally pick up casual work. Her Dutch parents raised her to understand value for money, always joking that the Dutch know how to be frugal and can recognise a bargain anywhere. Mila would like to get a regular part time job so that she can buy the things she needs without worrying about always looking for what she calls ‘goedkope’ or bargain items.

Mila has found it difficult to get jobs and wonders whether it is because of her disability, her patchy work history, or just that she is a single woman. She has found prospective employers interested in whether she has a partner or is married. Mila finds this very frustrating as she is a strong independent woman.

Matthew is in his late 20s, is unemployed and receives unemployment benefits.

He has participated in several JobActive programs, including Work for the Dole, to try and help him get a job but to no avail. He is currently attending GCBH Employment and Training Programs to try and improve his oral and written communication skills to see if this will help him get and keep a job.

Matthew has a mental health condition – depression and anxiety – which he knows is very common for men aged between 16 and 24. He wants to shake off what he and many others call the ‘black dog’ and finds that his cycles of depression seriously impact his ability to get and keep a job.

Matthew has many friends in his social network that help and support him when he needs it, however he still often feels isolated and alone due to his anxiety and finds it hard to trust people.

Maria is in her early 20s. She is a single mother with a young son attending kindergarten.

Maria has been looking for work for about a year and is frustrated by the work that is available to her. Maria has dreamed of working in robotics since secondary school when science was her favourite subject but knows that getting work will be hard because she has to consider child care for her son because he is so young. She needs something that gives her flexibility.

Maria has a small group of friends that can support her, but evening work has always been a challenge. She has limited people that she can ask to support her at night.

Maria had her son when she was very young and has not done any further study since leaving school. Maria was brought up to believe that if you work hard and dream big anything is possible. She picks up the occasional shift at a local café supporting lunch trade, but she knows this is not what she wants to do in life. However, she feels like she is trapped in a cycle because she doesn’t have the money she needs to pursue her dreams and is currently getting by week to week on the small amount she receives from the government and occasional work.

She would love to go onto further study and learn about robotics. This has always interested her and after her son is in bed, she spends her evenings reading about robotics and engineering advances. She has come to GCBH to seek employment and educational advice and support.
