1 Intersectionality and identity

Work through the following scenario on intersectionality and identity. This is not an assessment. In each step, select the action you would take. You can try this activity as many times as you like.

Trisha has been instructed by management to develop and roll out a new uniform for all staff who deal directly with customers. She is already under pressure and feels overwhelmed by this addition to her workload so she is keen to get it out of the way as soon as possible.

Should she start by:

  • Consulting staff on what kind of uniform might best suit their needs?
  • Researching uniform suppliers and get a quote for them to manage the design process?

Trisha decides to consult staff about what kind of uniform might suit them. She makes up a survey and posts it on the intranet.

After a week there is very little response to the survey. Should Trisha:

  • Go and speak to affected staff directly?
  • Go ahead and research suppliers?

Trisha finds a uniform supplier that is going to design a uniform consistent with GCBH branding for a reasonable price. After a couple of weeks they send her some samples, should she:

  • Show the samples to staff and ask them for feedback before getting sign off from management?
  • Show the samples to management and get sign off?

Trisha makes a list of key staff who will be required to wear the uniform.

Should she:

  • Select for diversity?
  • Ask people at random?

Trisha puts the uniform samples on display in the lunchroom and asks staff for feedback.

Jinni, from reception, wears a hijab. She asks Trisha what colour hijab she should wear to match the uniform.

Should she:

  • Integrate Jinni’s question with the rest of the staff feedback.
  • Research GCBH’s uniform policy.

Management signs off on the uniform and praises Trisha for getting it done so quickly. She places samples of the uniform in the lunchroom with a sign letting staff know that it will be compulsory in one month’s time and outlining the process to order their size.

Jinni, from reception, wears a hijab. She asks Trisha what colour hijab she should wear to match the uniform.

Should Trisha:

  • Research GCBH’s uniform policy.
  • Tell Jinni that she shouldn’t wear the hijab at work anymore.

Trisha selects people from the list that represent the diversity of staff who will be required to wear a uniform in terms of role, cultural background, disability and gender.

Trisha feels overwhelmed by the list of requirements. Should she:

  • Take the staff feedback on board.
  • Decide it’s too hard and ignore the feedback from staff.

Trisha goes into the lunchroom and has a conversation with a handful of people. They don’t have much to say about the uniform.

  • Trisha goes ahead and researches suppliers.

Trisha goes to check the uniform policy and discovers that there isn’t one.

Should Trisha:

  • Follow up with management about Jinni’s question.
  • Tell Jinni that she shouldn’t wear the hijab at work anymore.

Management says that wearing a hijab is part of Jinni’s religious freedom and the new uniform will have to be adjusted to suit her.

Plus there have been a number of complaints about the new uniform and its lack of suitability for certain roles.

Management advises Trisha to go back to the start and make a list of key staff that represent the diversity of the staff who will be required to wear the uniform and carry out a thorough staff consultation.

  • Trisha makes a list of staff, selecting for diversity.

Trisha tells Jinni that it’s important for GCBH to present a consistent image to the community. She suggests that Jinni give up wearing a hijab so she can appear more welcoming.

Jinni talks to her teammates about Trisha’s decision, she is upset and confused. Jinni’s team don’t like the uniform and are angry that they weren’t consulted. They encourage Jinni to seek advice from the union and the Victorian Equal Opportunity and Human Rights Commission.

When the time comes to implement the new uniform a number of staff refuse to wear it.

  • While Trisha’s choices have made this process more efficient, it has not been inclusive. Try again.

Trisha’s conversations lead to a short list of requirements to pass on to the designer, which includes culturally appropriate options for staff who wear hijabs.

She then uses this information to select a designer that can meet everyone’s needs.

When the time comes to start wearing the new uniform, staff already know what is happening and they all show up looking great.

  • Congratulations! You have helped Trisha take an inclusive approach. By integrating the questions and feedback from both management and staff, everyone is happy with the new uniforms.
