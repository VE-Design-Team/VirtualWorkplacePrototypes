2. The power of listening to create gender equality in the workplace.

The most powerful thing you can do to create change in the workplace is to listen to your team. That is the sign of good leadership. It sounds like a contradiction doesn’t it? But let me explain.

When you are working towards gender equity, the best thing that you can do is to consult with the people in your organisation. You may have a clear vision of what you want to achieve in your workplace. You may have years of expertise in fostering a good culture, and a progressive and supportive workplace. You may deeply understand the complexities of gender equity. And you may even have done extensive research to develop a clear path towards achieving gender equity that’s based on solid international evidence. All of these things may be in place and your goals may be clear to you, which is great.

However, you will never achieve your goals if you don’t bring your team along on the journey.

As a HR practitioner, I have played my part many times in implementing significant cultural change, and the value of consultation is something that I always come back to. 

Consultation is the act of listening to the people in your organisation and understanding what concerns them, confuses them, and what they see as success. This information will help you to develop a vision of gender equity in the workplace that is specific and relevant to your organisation. It also means that people will understand how to contribute to this plan. Consultation can also be a useful forum to empower people with clear information and practical tools to act.

The way in which you consult depends on the size and culture of your organisation, as well as the resources of your own team. There are many creative approaches that you can consider, such as:

  • recruit champions who can have conversations, listen and share knowledge with their colleagues. Their gathered knowledge will inform the development of your gender equity plan.

  • go to where your people are. Join in regular team meetings and make gender equity a part of everyday conversation.

  • convene a working group with representatives from across the organisation. This group should have a clear mandate and leadership support.

  • engage your leadership to go out and ask questions. Be sure to have a defined timeline for their response so you can get back to the organisation with timely answers.

  • In the end, the best way to achieve change is to empower the people in your organisation with a voice, knowledge and agency.

At the end of the consultation process you may find that the best strategies don’t differ too much from what you would have come up with regardless. But you have just been through a much more valuable experience. The whole organisation is now considering what gender equity means to them, and they will be more invested in the plan to achieve that success.

Everyone will know how they can contribute and how they will all benefit.

Through respectful listening and clear information, you have engaged the whole organisation to start taking this collective journey towards gender equity.

Phillip Martin,
HR Manager
