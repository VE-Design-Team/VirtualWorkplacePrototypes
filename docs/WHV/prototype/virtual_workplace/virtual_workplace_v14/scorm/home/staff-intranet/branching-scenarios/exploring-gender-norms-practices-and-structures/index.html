2 Gender norms, practices and structures in the workplace

Work through the following scenario on gender norms, practices and structures in the workplace. This is not an assessment. In each step, select the action you would take. You can try this activity as many times as you like.

Glenn, is an employment advisor at GCBH, he is meeting with a new client, Maria, to assist her journey to employment. Glenn should start by:

  • Explaining the standard procedure for looking for work and advising Maria on her responsibilities.
  • Asking Maria about what she has done so far to find a job and what kind of work she is looking for.

Maria sits silently while Glenn talks about the different websites that jobseekers can access to find work. He notices her silence and wonders about how motivated she is to find work. Glenn should:

  • Ask Maria about what she has done so far to find a job and what kind of work she is looking for.
  • Continue explaining to Maria about how important it is to find work and contribute to society.

Maria says she has been looking for work for about a year, since her son started kindergarten. She is interested in part-time work that will give her the flexibility to be with her son. Glenn should:

  • Ask about Maria’s education and qualifications.
  • Ask about Maria’s interest in the kind of work women usually do and suggest that she apply for work in hospitality, retail, childcare or cleaning.

Maria explains that she finished high school but didn’t do any further education because she has been home with her son. Her favourite subject was science because she is interested in robotics. Glenn should:

  • Ask if Maria is interested in an apprenticeship that will allow her to obtain an electrical engineering qualification while she works.
  • Ask about Maria’s interest in the kind of work women usually do such as hospitality, retail, childcare or cleaning.

Maria is silent for a moment, she says she has applied for retail jobs, and has done some hospitality work but she is not interested in working with food or with children and doesn’t want to clean people’s homes. She says that what she really wants to do is make robots. Glenn should:

  • Tell Maria she needs to be open minded about the kind of work she is looking for.
  • Ask if Maria is interested in an apprenticeship that will allow her to obtain an electrical engineering qualification while she works.

Maria brightens, she asks where she can find out more about the apprenticeship program. Glenn tells Maria there is an information session at 7.30pm the following Tuesday. Maria frowns, she explains that it will be difficult for her to find childcare with only a week’s notice and 7.30pm is her son’s bedtime so it won’t be possible to bring him. Glenn frowns, most of the people interested in the apprenticeship program are young men, the timing has never been a problem before. “Oh that’s a shame” says Glenn “the information session is a great opportunity to meet potential employers.” Maria is disappointed as it sounds like a chance to network and learn more. Glenn should:

  • Give information.
  • Offer funds for babysitting.

Glenn tells Maria that if she wants to get a job that will allow her to care for her son she really needs to stick to female dominated industries that have the most flexibility in hours as they offer a range of casual shifts. He acknowledges that it isn’t fair but that most workplaces do not support flexible work options and she needs to be realistic.

  • Glenn is not listening to Maria and his advice is adhering to traditional, stereotypical gender unequal norms, practices, and structures. Try again.

Glenn gives Maria some written information about the program, shows her the website and wishes her luck with her application.

  • While Glenn is providing information, his role as an employment advisor is to assist Maria in finding relevant employment. This means listening, considering her circumstances and finding ways to best help her on her journey to employment. Go back.

Glenn lets Maria know that GCBH has some brokerage funding to help people with the costs of finding employment. These funds are usually for things like tools, uniforms or other work appropriate clothing. He offers to reimburse Maria for the cost of a babysitter so that she can attend the information session. Maria thanks Glenn for the offer but says she would prefer to be home for her son’s bed time. Glenn gives Maria some written information on the apprenticeship program and offers to make a recording of Tuesday’s session so that she can listen to it when her son is at school. Glenn asks Maria if she would like him to take note of any employers at the session who might be interested in an electrical engineering apprentice and make an introduction on her behalf. Maria thanks Glenn and leaves feeling excited about the possibilities ahead of her.

  • Congratulations! You have helped Glenn avoid traditional gender norms, practices, and structures, to listen to what Maria wants to pursue in work, consider her circumstances, and provide her with the resources to get started.
