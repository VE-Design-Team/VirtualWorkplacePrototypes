3 Communicating gender equity messages

Work through the following scenario on communicating gender equity messages. This is not an assessment. In each step, select the action you would take. You can try this activity as many times as you like.

David is the Community Programs Manager at GCBH, he has been tasked with organising a community event to promote young women’s participation in sport, particularly those from a Culturally and Linguistically Diverse (CALD) background. The first thing David should do is:

  • Convene a working group.
  • Go ahead and start organising alone.

David invites a small group of stakeholders from schools, sporting clubs and community organisations to help him put together the event. Together they decide on a date, time and location for the event – one of the local schools offers to host it making the event more accessible and cheaper to run. David starts putting together a flier, he adds all the event details and finishes it off with a picture of a sports model. Should he:

  • Send the flier to the working group for their feedback.
  • Finish the flier himself and send it out.

David decides that convening a working group for such a small event is a waste of time. He decides on a date, time and location for the event and starts working on a flier. He adds all the event details and finishes it off with a picture of a sports model. Should he:

  • Send the flier to a couple of his stakeholders for feedback.
  • Finish the flier himself and send it out.

David gets feedback that the flier might be intimidating to young women who aren’t already physically active. Some stakeholders also let him know that the language is too complex and that if he wants young women from CALD backgrounds to come, the images should reflect more diversity. David should:

  • Ignore the feedback as he hasn’t got time to change it right now.
  • Take the feedback and create a new draft of the flier.

Aiming to get the event sorted out as quickly as possible, David finishes off the flier and sends it out. He immediately gets feedback that the flier is not very engaging and is unlikely to attract young women because the language is too complex and the images don’t reflect diversity. David should:

  • Ignore the feedback as some people just like to be critical.
  • Take the feedback, change the flier and send it out again.

David ignores the feedback and uses the flier to promote the event. After a week or so he notices there have been no RSVPs, he begins to wonder if he should cancel.

  • By not consulting with key stakeholders, David has not properly considered the intersectional needs of the community. Try again.

David takes the feedback on board and creates a flier that reflects a more diverse group of young women. The stakeholders are happy to help him promote the event, since they have been involved in organising it. After a week the event is already half full and on its way to success.

  • Congratulations! You have helped David create a successful flier and event by consulting with key stakeholders to find out what would be most appropriate and how to meet the intersectional needs of the community.
