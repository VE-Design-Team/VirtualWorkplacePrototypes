6 Collecting primary data

Work through the following scenario on collecting primary data. This is not an assessment. In each step, select the action you would take. You can try this activity as many times as you like.

You have been contracted by Trisha the Business Services Manager at GCBH, to carry out an evaluation of GCBH programs. You start by collecting all the data you can find from across the organisation. What is your next step?

  • Review the data you have collected to see if there are any gaps.
  • Convene a cross organisational working group and lead them to review the data together with an intersectional lens.

You notice that the quality of data is quite variable across the organisation and it is difficult to tell who is being reached by GCBH programs. Do you:

  • Convene a cross organisational working group.
  • Develop a policy on data collection and send it to all managers by email.

The working group notices large gaps in the data and variability in collection across the organisation. They also notice that it is impossible to know who the programs are reaching and how this compares to the makeup of the local community because that data hasn’t been captured. They suggest that a data collection policy be developed and Paul the Employment & Training Manager offers to work with you on it. Do you:

  • Develop a policy on data collection and send it to all managers by email.
  • Develop a policy on data collection and invite the working group to share the data collection policy and offer to present it to their teams.

You send out the new data collection policy to all managers but hear nothing back. After speaking to a couple of managers you discover that they didn’t open the email you sent and haven’t passed the information along to their teams. Do you:

  • Offer to go and present to their teams on the new policy.
  • Give up on changing policy, because it’s clear that no one really cares about good data collection anyway, Accept that you did what you could and move to the next stage.

You present the data collection policy to a number of teams across the organisation and statistics on the makeup of the local community to demonstrate the importance of assessing GCBH’s reach. If you did this with the support of a working group you notice a significant rise in the quality of data collection. If you developed the policy without consultation you notice a modest rise in the quality of data collection.

  • Now you decide to collect some data yourself to start assessing the impact of GCBH programs.

You now turn your attention to assessing the impact of GCBH programs. Do you:

  • Convene focus groups with managers, project workers and clients, all together?
  • Convene separate focus groups for managers, project workers and clients?

You convene a focus group and invite everyone you can think of who might have been involved with a GCBH program – project workers, managers, clients, other members of the community. You notice that the clients in the room are quiet while the managers do a lot of the talking. You also notice that there are three men in the group that do most of the talking. You write up the data and then:

  • Run another series of separated focus groups.
  • Accept that you did what you could and move to the next stage.

You convene a series of focus groups for different stakeholders:

  • managers
  • project workers
  • even separate groups for male and female clients

Every group gives you different information about what is and isn’t working in GCBH programs. You discover:

  • that the employment outcomes for women accessing the service have not been as sustainable as for men,
  • that people from CALD backgrounds have a lower employment rate,
  • the majority of GCBH programs are accessed by Australian born women and men from low SES backgrounds.

You take all this data and develop a presentation. Do you:

  • Deliver the presentation at the next all staff meeting.
  • Deliver the presentation to the working group for their endorsement.

You put together all the findings from the data you have gathered across the organisation and the data you have collected for yourself. You develop a presentation that supports that the organisation is doing fine. The data says nothing about who is being reached by GCBH programs or what impact they have. Several of the managers express frustration at the lack of clarity in the results and ask why you didn’t consult with them at the outset.

  • By not providing opportunities for the rest of the focus group to voice their experiences and only relying on the feedback of three vocal managers and your own research, you have not been able to provide a clear picture of how the GCBH programs are running and the impact they are having. Try again.

You deliver the findings from your evaluation – the focus groups input and the newly improved agency data – alongside statistics for Gentown Local Government Area, to the all staff meeting. It clearly shows that there are significant marginalised groups that are not being reached by GCBH programs. Managers and staff at the meeting get defensive and claim that you are criticising their work. They question your methods and demand that the findings be suppressed.

  • You should first present your findings to the working group to ensure you are adequately addressing the pros and cons of the GCBH programs brought up in the focus groups. Their support will help you communicating more effectively to the rest of the staff. Go back.

You deliver the findings from your evaluation – the focus groups input and the newly improved agency data – alongside statistics for Gentown Local Government Area, to the project working group. It clearly shows that there are significant marginalised groups that are not being reached by GCBH programs. The working group endorses the findings and encourages you to deliver the presentation to all staff and the data you have collected becomes the basis for an organisation-wide inclusion strategy. As all staff were involved in the consultation process they will be receptive to taking on board the findings.

  • Congratulations! By listening and consulting the different stakeholders in separate focus groups, you have provided people with the opportunity to comfortably speak up and engage with the process. As a result, you have been able to present findings relevant to GCBH
